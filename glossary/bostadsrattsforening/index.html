Bostadsrättsförening

Bostadsrättsförening Sweden – Housing Cooperative Explained

A bostadsrättsförening (BRF — housing cooperative) is the legal entity that owns a Swedish apartment building, while its members hold bostadsrätter (cooperative rights) entitling them to occupy specific flats. When you buy an apartment in Sweden, you are typically buying the bostadsrätt — the cooperative right — not real property in the legal sense.

The BRF is managed by a board (styrelse) elected annually by members at the general meeting (föreningsstämma). The board handles building maintenance, negotiates service contracts, manages the renovation fund, and sets the monthly fee (månadsavgift). Members are required to pay the månadsavgift regardless of whether they occupy or rent out the unit.

Each BRF has a debt level (belåning) representing the total loan burden allocated across all units. This shared debt (föreningens lån) is critical: buyers must add their share of the BRF's debt to the purchase price to understand the total implied cost. A BRF with high debt per m² will either charge higher månadsavgifter or will need to raise them in the future.

Key Facts

Ownership structureBRF owns building; members own bostadsrätt
Monthly feeMånadsavgift — paid by all members
Key debt metricFöreningens lån per m² — target < SEK 5,000
Annual reportÅrsredovisning — essential due diligence
GovernanceBoard (styrelse) + annual general meeting

The BRF's annual accounts and budget are the most important due diligence documents. Key metrics include: debt per m² (ideally below SEK 5,000/m²), the ratio of månadsavgift to comparable market rents (above 70% is a warning sign), and whether the maintenance plan (underhållsplan) is adequately funded.

Buyers should request and read the BRF's senaste årsredovisning (latest annual report) and the minutes of recent general meetings. These are provided as part of the sale documentation for registered bostadsrätter but should be reviewed carefully — a BRF facing large upcoming maintenance costs or financial stress can make the apparent purchase price misleadingly cheap.

⚠ Common MistakeNot calculating the total implied cost (purchase price + your share of BRF debt) when comparing bostadsrätter. A lower headline price with high BRF debt can be more expensive in total than a higher price with low debt.
💡 Expert TipUse the BRF's NE supplement to the årsredovisning to find the debt per lägenhet (per unit). Divide total debt by total m² to get debt per m². Combine this with the månadsavgift per m² for a complete picture of the cost of ownership.

Frequently Asked Questions

Who is responsible for repairs inside a bostadsrätt?

The member (bostadsrättshavare) is responsible for the interior of their unit, including non-load-bearing walls, flooring, and appliances. The BRF is responsible for the building structure, common areas, and major systems.

Can a BRF go bankrupt?

Yes. If a BRF cannot service its debts, it may be wound up and the building sold. Members lose their bostadsrätter. This is rare but has occurred. BRF debt and financial health due diligence is therefore critical.

Can I rent out my bostadsrätt?

You need the BRF's approval (andrahandsuthyrning). BRFs are generally required to grant approval for valid reasons (work, study, cohabitation trial) but can set conditions and time limits.
