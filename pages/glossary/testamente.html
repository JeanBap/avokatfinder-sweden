Testamente

Definition: A Swedish will that specifies how a person's property should be distributed after death.

Overview

A testamente (will) in Sweden is a legal document in which a person declares how their property should be distributed after death. It must be created while the testator has full legal capacity and understanding.

Swedish law recognizes both handwritten (holographic) wills and wills witnessed by two independent witnesses. A testamente must clearly identify the testator, heirs, and the distribution of assets.

The will must comply with Swedish formalities: it should be dated, signed, and either witnessed or notarized. Failure to follow proper procedures can result in the will being declared invalid.

In Sweden, a testamente can designate an executor (testamentexekutor) to carry out the wishes. It can also contain specific bequests, charitable donations, or conditional inheritances.

Swedish law also provides rules of intestate succession if no valid will exists. These statutory rules ensure a default distribution to spouse, children, and other relatives.

Key Facts

  • Must be created by testator with legal capacity
  • Should be dated and signed
  • Can be handwritten or formally witnessed
  • Can designate an executor to manage the estate
  • Can include specific bequests and charitable donations

Common Mistake

Watch out: Creating an informal will without proper witnesses, which may be rejected by courts or contested by heirs.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is required for a valid Swedish testamente?

The will must be dated, signed by the testator, and either handwritten or witnessed by two independent adults.

Can I change my testamente?

Yes, you can create a new will or an amendment (codicil) at any time while you have legal capacity.

What if I die without a testamente?

Swedish intestate succession laws apply, distributing the estate to surviving spouses, children, and other relatives in a statutory order.
